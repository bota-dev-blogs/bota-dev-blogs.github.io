## The Short Answer

The most useful way to think about modern speech AI is not "ASR versus audio LLMs." It is **transcription versus interaction**.

[Automatic speech recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech_recognition), or ASR, is still the layer that turns speech into searchable, auditable text. Large audio language models, often called LALMs, add the ability to reason over sound, speakers, music, noise, and timing. Real-time speech-to-speech systems go further: they decide when to listen, when to interrupt, when to speak, and how to keep a live conversation moving.

ASR is not disappearing. It is becoming one evidence layer inside a larger voice-agent stack.

![Real-time speech AI stack](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-2/01-article-summary.gif)

This article maps that stack through several public reference points: Alibaba Cloud Model Studio's [speech-to-text model documentation](https://www.alibabacloud.com/help/en/model-studio/asr-model/), the [Qwen-Audio realtime voice model](https://www.alibabacloud.com/help/en/model-studio/qwen-audio-realtime-user-guides), NVIDIA's [Audio Flamingo Next](https://huggingface.co/nvidia/audio-flamingo-next-hf), OpenMOSS [MOSS-Audio-8B-Instruct](https://ai.gitcode.com/OpenMOSS/MOSS-Audio-8B-Instruct), Qwen's [Qwen3.5-LiveTranslate](https://qwen.ai/blog?id=qwen3.5-livetranslate), and the [Qwen3.5-Omni technical report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15804).

The goal is not to rank models. The goal is to answer a product question: **if you are building a voice agent, which layer should own recognition, audio understanding, response timing, and real-world action?**

Focused guides cover [streaming ASR](/blogs/real-time-asr-2026-streaming-recognition/), [long-audio models](/blogs/audio-language-models-long-audio-understanding/), [speech-to-speech translation](/blogs/speech-to-speech-translation-live-voice/), and [real-time voice-agent APIs](/blogs/qwen-audio-3-realtime-voice-agents/).

## Why This Debate Is Suddenly Confusing

Voice AI used to have a clean pipeline:

1. ASR transcribes audio.
2. A text LLM reasons over the transcript.
3. [Text-to-speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech_synthesis) speaks the answer.

That pipeline is easy to debug. It also exposes useful artifacts: transcript text, timestamps, prompts, tool arguments, and generated speech.

Newer systems blur those boundaries. A real-time model may receive audio directly, maintain conversation state, generate text and speech events, perform turn detection, and call tools through an event loop. A large audio-language model may answer questions about a recording without first producing a full transcript. A live translation model may combine speech, vision, terminology hints, speaker identity, and speech synthesis in one streaming system.

The result is a tempting but misleading narrative: if a model can hear, maybe ASR disappears.

It does not. What disappears is the assumption that a transcript is the whole product.

## Four Layers, Four Different Jobs

Speech products often compress everything into the phrase "voice AI." That hides the real engineering choices.

ASR answers: **what was said?**

A LALM can answer: **what evidence and events are present in this audio?**

A real-time speech model answers: **how should the system participate right now?**

A control plane answers: **what is the assistant allowed to execute?**

Those are separate jobs. Treating them as one model choice can hide important product boundaries.

## ASR Is The Precision Layer

ASR remains the right center of gravity when the output needs to be a durable record. Meeting transcription, call analysis, captions, subtitle editing, searchable archives, legal review, accessibility, and customer support QA all need explicit text.

As reviewed on July 23, 2026, Alibaba Cloud's speech-to-text model page makes this distinction visible. It lists real-time and offline recognition models, including `fun-asr-realtime`, `fun-asr`, and Qwen omni speech models, with differences across API mode, hotwords, speaker diarization, language coverage, emotion recognition, and maximum audio duration.

Those details matter more than model hype. A practical ASR layer gives a product:

- Searchable transcripts.
- Sentence and word timestamps.
- Domain vocabulary or hotword control.
- Speaker diarization when the product needs "who said what."
- Confidence and alternatives for review.
- A text artifact that can be stored, edited, cited, and audited.

The limitation is also clear. Text is a lossy representation of speech. It can omit hesitation, overlap, laughter, breath, room noise, timing, and vocal delivery. A transcript may contain the word "fine" while the surrounding audio suggests a more complicated interpretation.

So the useful claim is not "ASR solves voice." The useful claim is: **ASR is the evidence layer when exact words matter.**

## LALMs Are The Interpretation Layer

Audio Flamingo Next is a useful reference because its public model card, reviewed on July 23, 2026, presents it as an [audio-text-to-text](https://huggingface.co/tasks/audio-text-to-text) model. The card frames it around audio understanding, instruction following, audio QA, long-form audio, timestamp-aware prompts, speech, environmental sounds, and music.

That is a different product surface from conventional ASR. A user may ask:

- "What happened before the alarm?"
- "Which sound events occur around the spoken exchange?"
- "Summarize the argument in this meeting and point to rough timestamps."
- "What changes between these two recordings?"
- "Is this audio mostly speech, music, noise, or mixed?"

A transcript alone is too narrow for those questions.

MOSS-Audio-8B-Instruct sits in a similar category from an open audio-model direction. Its public page, reviewed on the same date, describes speech recognition, environmental sound analysis, music understanding, audio captioning, and time-aware question answering. Whether a builder chooses that exact model is less important than the pattern: audio models are becoming general-purpose listening engines.

![Model family map](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-2/02-model-family-map.gif)

The boundary is output and timing. If a model takes audio and returns text, it may be excellent for analysis, but it is not automatically a real-time voice agent. Something else still has to decide how to speak, when to stop, whether to revise, and what to do when the user interrupts.

## Live Translation Shows Why Real-Time Is Hard

Qwen3.5-LiveTranslate is interesting because simultaneous interpretation exposes the core real-time problem.

Translation is not just ASR plus machine translation plus TTS. A live interpreter has to decide how long to wait, which partial phrase is stable, how to handle cross-language word order, whether to revise displayed text, how to preserve terminology, and when speech output should begin.

The Qwen3.5-LiveTranslate announcement describes a model built on Qwen3.5-Omni, with expanded language coverage, low-latency streaming, voice cloning, hotword handling, visual context, and a "Readable Unit" approach for more stable simultaneous output. The product lesson is broader than translation: **real-time speech systems need pacing rules, not just better recognition.**

If the system waits too long, latency feels broken. If it speaks too soon, it may mistranslate because the sentence has not resolved. If it revises constantly, the interface jitters. If it never revises, early errors become permanent.

That is why live translation belongs in this discussion. It makes the hidden control problem visible.

## Speech-To-Speech Moves Timing Into The Model Loop

The [Qwen3.5-Omni technical report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15804) is a useful design reference because it describes an omni model rather than a narrow transcription system. The public abstract highlights 256k context, large-scale audio-visual training, a Hybrid Attention Mixture-of-Experts framework, long audio/video understanding, and a Thinker-Talker architecture.

The important idea for builders is the separation of responsibilities.

The Thinker handles multimodal understanding and text-level reasoning. The Talker handles streaming speech generation. Qwen3.5-Omni also introduces ARIA, or Adaptive Rate Interleave Alignment, to dynamically align text and speech units when tokenization rates do not naturally match.

That sounds technical because it is technical. But the product implication is simple: speech-to-speech AI is a **rate-control system**. It has to keep words, meaning, prosody, and audio playback synchronized while the user is still talking.

A native speech model may feel more natural than ASR -> LLM -> TTS when it can use timing, [prosody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)), interruptions, and conversation state inside the same loop.

The tradeoff is observability. A modular pipeline exposes intermediate artifacts. A native real-time model may be smoother but harder to debug. In production, that usually argues for a hybrid stack rather than a single opaque endpoint.

## The Missing Layer Is The Control Plane

A real-time voice agent is not only a listener and a speaker. It can act.

![Tool-Using Speech Needs A Control Loop](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-2/03-voice-agent-control-loop.gif)

It may schedule a meeting, send a message, update a CRM, change a device setting, summarize a call, buy a ticket, modify a workflow, or route a support case. At that point, the model's interpretation is no longer just language. It becomes authority.

This is where many demos are misleading. A demo can let a model speak fluently. A product needs to decide whether the model is allowed to do anything.

A practical control plane should keep three channels separate:

| Channel | What It Tracks | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Evidence | Transcript, audio timing, model confidence, retrieved context | Prevents uncertain recognition from becoming confident action |
| Timing | Turns, pauses, overlap, interruption, cancellation | Keeps the assistant responsive without acting too early |
| Authority | Tool schemas, permissions, confirmations, logs | Separates model intent from real-world execution |

Alibaba Cloud's Qwen-Audio real-time guide makes this API shape concrete: real-time audio systems use persistent WebSocket sessions, streaming audio input, streaming text/audio output, turn detection modes, response cancellation, conversation context, and function calling. That is not a normal request-response transcript API. It is an event-driven state machine with a model inside it.

## How To Choose The Right Speech AI Pattern

The architecture choice should follow the product's primary risk.

![Choose A Center Of Gravity](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-1/03-speech-architecture-choice.gif)

| Product Goal | Better Starting Point | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Captions, transcripts, search, compliance | ASR-first | The durable artifact is text with timing and reviewability |
| Audio QA, long recording analysis, sound understanding | LALM-first | The product needs to reason over more than words |
| Live interpreter, tutor, assistant, companion | Real-time speech-to-speech | The user experience depends on timing, turn-taking, and speech output |
| Calendar, payments, devices, enterprise workflows | Hybrid stack with control plane | The model can affect external state and needs authorization |

The practical answer is: **ASR remains essential for many voice agents, but it should not be assumed to be the only speech layer.**

## Evaluation Should Match The Stack

If a benchmark collapses the whole system into one score, it hides failure modes.

![A Hybrid Stack Preserves Observability](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-2/04-hybrid-speech-observability.gif)

ASR should be evaluated on [word error rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_error_rate), timestamp drift, hotword accuracy, endpointing, accents, noise, multilingual behavior, and [speaker diarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speaker_diarisation).

Audio understanding should be evaluated on grounded audio QA, event timing, long-audio memory, multi-speaker reasoning, paralinguistic cues, and hallucination under ambiguous sound.

Real-time speech should be evaluated on first-audio latency, interruption stop time, barge-in recovery, duplex behavior, partial response stability, and latency under load.

Tool-using voice agents should be evaluated on argument validity, permission violations, confirmation behavior, audit logs, and recovery after uncertainty.

One metric deserves more attention: abstention. A voice agent that says "I did not catch that" at the right time is often safer than a fluent model that turns weak audio into a confident command.

## What Builders Should Take Away

![Design questions for voice agents](/media/gifs/realtime-speech-ai-control-stack/pipeline-1/01-design-questions.gif)

A robust near-term pattern is not "one model replaces everything." It is a layered system with clear boundaries.

Use ASR when exact words, timestamps, search, accessibility, audit, and review matter.

Use audio-language models when the product needs to understand sound as sound, not just words as text.

Use real-time speech-to-speech when latency, interruption, tone, and conversational timing are part of the task.

Use a control plane whenever the assistant can call tools or change external state.

The future of voice AI is not ASR disappearing. It is ASR becoming one evidence channel inside a broader real-time speech system.

That is less dramatic than saying speech models have replaced transcription. It is also a more defensible basis for production voice-agent design.
