## The Customer Is A Software Company, Not The Person Wearing The Device

An AI wearable can look like a consumer product. A person clips it to clothing, presses a button, records a conversation, and later receives a transcript or summary.

But there is a different business hidden behind that familiar surface.

Instead of selling a recording app to individuals, a company can sell **physical conversation capture as infrastructure** to other software companies. The software company embeds the device, mobile synchronization, recording API, transcription, and event delivery into its own product. Its users may never interact with the infrastructure provider's brand.

This is a B2B2C model: an infrastructure company serves a software business, and that software business delivers the final experience to an employee, clinician, lawyer, investor, or field worker.

The underlying product need is explained in [why AI agents need offline conversation context](/blogs/offline-context-infrastructure-ai-agents/). The companion [AI wearable developer-platform guide](/blogs/ai-wearable-developer-platform-ble-wifi-apis/) covers BLE, Wi-Fi provisioning, SDKs, recording APIs, and webhooks.

![The B2B market for offline conversation APIs](/media/gifs/ai-wearables-offline-conversation-apis/pipeline-2/01-article-summary.gif)

The category is best understood as **Hardware-as-a-Service plus an API platform**. The hardware acquires real-world context. The API makes that context programmable. Managed operations keep the physical fleet usable after launch.

The distinction matters because the buyer, product, economics, and support burden are all different from those of a consumer recorder.

## The Product Is Really Two Products

An offline conversation platform sells two tightly coupled products.

The first is a **capture system**:

- A wearable or portable recording device.
- Mobile or direct network synchronization.
- Battery, storage, firmware, and device identity.
- Provisioning, replacement, and fleet state.
- A reliable path from a physical event to durable audio.

The second is a **developer and operations platform**:

- Recording, device, and user APIs.
- SDKs for embedding setup and synchronization.
- Webhooks or events for recording lifecycle changes.
- Speech recognition, speaker processing, summaries, and structured outputs.
- Storage, retention, access control, and audit behavior.
- Administrative tools for devices and customer accounts.

Selling only the device leaves the customer to build the software platform. Selling only the API leaves the customer to source, integrate, provision, and support hardware. The category exists because many B2B software companies want the combined outcome but do not want to become hardware companies.

## Which Software Companies Are The Natural Buyers?

The most promising customers share one property: **a valuable workflow changes after an offline conversation, but the product cannot currently observe that conversation reliably**.

![One Platform Serves Distinct Workflow Owners](/media/gifs/ai-wearables-offline-conversation-apis/pipeline-2/03-vertical-workflows.gif)

That definition is more useful than a list of industries. It identifies where the infrastructure creates new product value rather than adding another source of transcripts.

### AI CRM And Sales Enablement

Sales systems know what was entered into the CRM, sent by email, or discussed on a recorded video call. They are weaker during site visits, conferences, store visits, customer lunches, and other face-to-face interactions.

An embedded capture system can help a sales product draft follow-ups, recover objections, identify competitors, update contact history, and suggest next steps.

The buyer is not purchasing audio for its own sake. It is purchasing better CRM completeness and less manual data entry. The product must therefore connect each conversation to the correct account, contact, opportunity, and salesperson.

A strong fit has three characteristics:

- Face-to-face conversations occur frequently.
- Important details are currently lost or entered late.
- The existing software can convert context into a measurable workflow outcome.

### AI Chief Of Staff And Enterprise Productivity

AI chief-of-staff products promise to organize decisions, commitments, and follow-up across a user's work. Their digital integrations may cover calendars, email, documents, and online meetings, but executive work also happens while traveling, walking a site, or talking in an office.

Offline conversation context can fill that gap. The product can reconcile what was said with the calendar, identify unresolved decisions, and prepare a review queue for tasks.

This segment values broad context, but it also creates a high privacy burden. The same device may hear strategic discussions, personnel matters, and irrelevant personal conversation. Selective capture, workspace boundaries, and deletion need to be part of the product promise.

### Healthcare Technology

Clinical and care workflows contain a large amount of spoken information. A conversation may need to become a draft note, patient instruction, coding input, or follow-up task.

Healthcare buyers care about more than transcription accuracy. They need a system that can support strict access control, traceable corrections, retention policy, deployment options, and professional review.

The economic value can be substantial when documentation consumes expensive staff time. The risk is also substantial because a missing negation, wrong medication, or swapped speaker can change meaning.

Infrastructure is attractive when the healthcare software company owns the clinical workflow but does not want to design a wearable, synchronization protocol, and device-support operation from scratch.

### Legal Technology

Legal products may need context from client interviews, depositions outside formal platforms, expert conversations, or internal matter discussions.

The workflow requires strong matter boundaries, source evidence, access logs, and control over derived summaries. A generic recording app can create a transcript, but it may not fit the identity, privilege, retention, and document-management model of the legal product.

Here, the API is as important as the microphone. The context must arrive inside the matter workspace with the correct policy and provenance.

### Knowledge Management And AI Workspaces

Knowledge tools are strongest when users actively upload or write information. Offline context creates a new ingestion channel for observations, interviews, brainstorming, and decisions that would otherwise disappear.

An AI workspace can turn those conversations into linked notes, project memories, concepts, and evidence. The useful output is not always a meeting summary. It may be a set of source-backed claims that can be connected to an existing canvas, research project, or knowledge graph.

This segment benefits from flexible schemas and retrieval APIs because every customer organizes knowledge differently.

### Financial Research And Private Markets

Investment teams and private-market researchers conduct expert calls, management conversations, site visits, and diligence interviews. The details can influence a thesis before they reach a formal memo.

Offline conversation infrastructure can make authorized recordings searchable, connect evidence to a company or research question, and let an analyst trace a claim back to the source discussion.

This is not a license to automate investment decisions from transcripts. The product value is faster synthesis and stronger evidence management. Access control, information barriers, retention, and review are central to the workflow.

### Vertical Software With Mobile Work

The category extends beyond the obvious knowledge-work examples. Property management, insurance claims, construction, logistics, inspections, home services, and other field workflows all create spoken context away from a desk.

The best opportunities are usually vertical. A general summary is less valuable than a domain object: a property issue, claim observation, safety concern, equipment symptom, or approved next step.

The vertical software company understands that object. The infrastructure provider supplies the physical and speech layer required to create it.

## A Simple Test For Customer Fit

A software company is a strong candidate when it can answer yes to most of these questions:

![Four Signals Of Customer Fit](/media/gifs/ai-wearables-offline-conversation-apis/pipeline-1/01-customer-fit-signals.gif)

1. Do important conversations happen away from the product's online meeting integrations?
2. Is missing context already causing manual work, slow follow-up, or incomplete records?
3. Does the company own a workflow where structured conversation output can trigger a useful next step?
4. Would users accept a dedicated capture device or embedded recording experience?
5. Can the product define when recording is appropriate and how consent is handled?
6. Is the expected value per active user high enough to support hardware and operations?
7. Does the company prefer to differentiate in its workflow rather than in device firmware and logistics?

A company that only wants a transcript viewer may be better served by a conventional recording app. Infrastructure becomes compelling when capture is a component inside a larger product.

## Why This Is Not A Consumer Recording Business

The two categories can use similar speech models while operating very differently.

### The Buyer

A consumer recorder is purchased by the person who records. An infrastructure platform is purchased by a product, engineering, operations, or enterprise team and deployed to that company's users.

### The Interface

A consumer product owns the app, transcript screen, subscription, and brand relationship. An infrastructure platform exposes SDKs, APIs, webhooks, administrative controls, and optional managed devices so another company can own the experience.

### The Success Metric

A consumer product may optimize recordings created, summaries viewed, or subscription retention. A B2B platform is evaluated on successful capture, time to durable context, API reliability, fleet health, workflow completion, and support burden.

### The Support Model

Consumer support handles individual purchases and app behavior. Infrastructure support may need to manage customer-specific provisioning, bulk shipments, replacements, device assignment, firmware rollout, and enterprise escalation.

### The Data Contract

A consumer application can define one transcript and note format. An embedded platform needs stable identifiers, lifecycle events, account isolation, configurable retention, and structured outputs that fit multiple products.

The infrastructure company is therefore closer to [Twilio's programmable communications](https://www.twilio.com/docs/voice) than to a standalone voice memo app. It supplies a capability that another product assembles into its own workflow.

## The Revenue Stack

The business model usually has several layers rather than one subscription.

### Device Revenue

Devices may be sold, leased, bundled into a contract, or subsidized against expected platform usage. The pricing choice changes cash flow, replacement expectations, and who owns the asset.

A one-time sale is simple but can make recurring support expensive. A lease or managed-device fee aligns revenue with fleet operations but places more capital and inventory responsibility on the provider.

### Platform Access

A base platform fee can cover tenant administration, SDK access, API credentials, dashboards, webhooks, and a committed support level. This is the software foundation of the relationship.

Pricing may be organized by organization, active user, active device, workspace, or deployment. The correct unit should follow customer value and remain predictable enough for a software company to build into its own pricing.

### Processing Usage

Speech recognition, diarization, summarization, storage, and data transfer create variable costs. A platform can charge per audio hour, recording minute, processed event, stored gigabyte, or package of credits.

Pure usage pricing is transparent but can make the customer's gross margin difficult to forecast. Commitments and volume tiers can make costs more predictable for both sides.

### Managed Hardware Operations

Fleet services can include inventory, provisioning, shipping, replacement, returns, recycling, firmware rollout, and end-user support.

This is not an accessory to the API. For many buyers, it is the reason to use a platform. A software company may be capable of calling an ASR endpoint but unwilling to operate thousands of physical devices.

Managed operations can be priced per active device, per shipment, through service tiers, or inside a broader enterprise contract.

### Enterprise Controls And Deployment

Larger customers may pay for custom retention, regional storage, dedicated infrastructure, private networking, single sign-on, audit exports, on-premises components, custom firmware, branded hardware, or negotiated support.

These capabilities increase contract value but can fragment the product if each customer receives a unique stack. The provider needs clear boundaries between configurable platform features and bespoke engineering.

## Hardware-as-a-Service Changes The Economics

Software margins are attractive partly because another customer can be served at low incremental cost. Hardware introduces inventory, manufacturing yield, shipping, loss, breakage, returns, and support.

An infrastructure provider must understand contribution margin across the whole account:

- Device manufacturing and financing cost.
- Freight, duties, warehousing, and fulfillment.
- Expected replacement and loss rates.
- Mobile synchronization and cloud infrastructure.
- ASR and model inference.
- Storage and egress.
- Customer support and fleet operations.
- Enterprise integration and security review.

The hardware can be a customer-acquisition mechanism, a profit center, or a subsidized endpoint for recurring platform revenue. Those are different strategies.

The strongest model aligns incentives: the provider earns more when devices remain active and useful, while the customer can predict the cost of delivering its workflow.

## Why A Software Company Buys Instead Of Builds

The decision is not simply hardware cost versus API cost. It is a comparison of organizational scope.

![Buy The Infrastructure, Keep The Workflow](/media/gifs/ai-wearables-offline-conversation-apis/pipeline-2/04-build-buy-boundary.gif)

Building requires capabilities in industrial design, manufacturing, firmware, Bluetooth or network synchronization, mobile development, security, cloud ingestion, speech processing, fleet management, logistics, and support.

Owning those capabilities can be rational when capture behavior is the product's central advantage or when data custody requires a unique architecture.

Buying is attractive when:

- The workflow and domain model are the real differentiators.
- Speed to market matters more than custom device design.
- Deployment volume does not justify a hardware organization.
- The company wants one accountable layer for device-to-cloud reliability.
- Enterprise customers expect operational support beyond an SDK.

The buyer should still preserve portability. Recording identifiers, exported audio, transcript schemas, and webhook semantics should not make it impossible to replace a model or migrate storage later.

## The Go-To-Market Motion Is Product-Led And Enterprise-Led

This category needs two sales motions at once.

Developers need a fast path to test the API, provision a small number of devices, receive recordings, and build a prototype. That resembles product-led API adoption.

Larger deployments require security review, operational planning, hardware forecasts, support agreements, data-processing terms, and rollout design. That resembles enterprise infrastructure sales.

A useful packaging structure often includes:

- A self-service or pilot tier for a small device fleet and standard APIs.
- A managed tier for inventory, replacements, support, and branding.
- An enterprise tier for deployment controls, volume commitments, and security requirements.

The names and boundaries vary. The principle is to let the buyer prove workflow value before committing to a large fleet, while making the operational path to scale explicit.

## What Buyers Should Evaluate

The best vendor evaluation follows the complete product experience.

![What Buyers Must Inspect](/media/gifs/ai-wearables-offline-conversation-apis/pipeline-1/03-buyer-evaluation.gif)

**Capture reliability:** Does the device make recording state clear? How does it recover after battery, storage, or connectivity failure?

**Integration depth:** Can the buyer embed provisioning, synchronization, and recording lifecycle into its own app and backend?

**Data ownership:** Can authorized customers export source audio, transcripts, metadata, and corrections in documented formats?

**Speech flexibility:** Can the buyer choose languages, domain vocabulary, model behavior, and structured output without rebuilding ingestion?

**Fleet operations:** Who owns inventory, replacement, firmware, returns, lost devices, and end-user support?

**Enterprise readiness:** Are tenancy, access control, audit, deletion, retention, regional deployment, and incident response clear?

**Unit economics:** What is the all-in cost per active device, recording hour, and successful workflow outcome?

**Exit path:** What happens to devices, stored data, and integrations if the relationship ends?

An impressive demo answers almost none of these questions. Infrastructure is valuable when it remains dependable after the first hundred devices, the first lost shipment, and the first customer-specific security review.

## The Strategic Position

The category sits between AI hardware, communications infrastructure, and vertical software.

Its durable advantage is unlikely to come from a transcription model alone. Speech models are improving quickly and can often be replaced. A deeper advantage can come from device reliability, deployment operations, integration quality, governance, and the accumulated knowledge required to make physical capture boring at scale.

That is also the category's central tension. The provider needs enough standardization to operate efficiently and enough flexibility to serve products with very different workflows.

The successful platform will not try to become the CRM, clinical record, legal workspace, or investment research system. It will make offline conversation context easy for those products to acquire and govern.

## Closing View

AI wearables do not have to be consumer gadgets. They can be endpoints in a B2B infrastructure network.

The natural buyers are software companies whose products become more valuable when they can observe authorized face-to-face conversations: sales, productivity, healthcare, legal, knowledge, finance, and vertical field applications.

The business model follows the technical reality. Device revenue pays for physical endpoints. Platform and usage fees pay for software and processing. Managed operations pay for the work that software companies do not want to own. Enterprise contracts pay for deployment controls and organizational assurance.

That combination is more complex than a recording subscription. It is also more defensible when the platform becomes the reliable bridge between real-world conversation and a high-value software workflow.
